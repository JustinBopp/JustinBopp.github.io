About Me

I am a Ph.D. student in Astrophysics & Computational Mathematics, Science and Engineering at Michigan State University, where I investigate compact objects, stellar explosions, and the extreme physics governing the most energetic events in the universe. My work sits at the intersection of high‑energy astrophysics, numerical simulations, and observational signatures of transient phenomena.

Before MSU, I completed my master’s degree at CUNY, working with Dr. Ore Gottlieb at the Center for Computational Astrophysics (Flatiron Institute). In that work, I analyzed 3D GRMHD simulations of collapsars—massive stars collapsing directly into black holes—with a focus on understanding how accretion‑disk dynamics generate outflows even in the absence of black hole spin.

Alongside research, I care deeply about mentorship, accessibility, and representation in STEM. Through programs such as AstroCom NYC, MSU Stellar Mentorship, and my outreach work organizing astronomy events, I aim to help build a more inclusive astrophysics community. My long‑term goal is a career in academia combining research, teaching, and community building.

Research

GRMHD Simulations of Non‑Spinning Black Hole Collapsars

My thesis research at the City University of New York (CUNY), conducted at the Center for Computational Astrophysics (CCA) at the Flatiron Institute under the supervision of Dr. Ore Gottlieb, investigates the dynamical evolution of collapsars—core-collapse supernovae that directly form black holes. Using the 3D GRMHD code HAMR, I simulate massive stellar collapse across a range of initial black hole natal spins to quantify how rotation influences accretion disk formation, magnetic flux accumulation, and the launching of outflows.

A central finding of my work is that even a non-spinning black hole can produce vigorous, subrelativistic, disk-powered outflows driven solely by the angular momentum of the nascent accretion disk. These outflows propagate through the stellar envelope and ultimately break out of the progenitor’s surface, unbinding the star as it expands into the interstellar medium. Notably, this is the first simulation to demonstrate stellar breakout from outflows powered exclusively by an accretion disk, offering new insight into the continuum between ordinary CCSNe and relativistic GRB-producing collapsars.

Through this work, I’ve developed expertise in high-performance computing, GRMHD simulations, and scientific communication. A related publication can be found here: Bopp & Gottlieb et al. (2025) .

Rubin Observatory DP0.2 Delegate

Under the guidance of Dr. Brian Morsony, I contributed to the Rubin Observatory’s Data Preview 0.2 by adapting and implementing a Color-Match Nearest Neighbor (CMNN) photometric redshift estimator for simulated LSST datasets. This project played a key role in preparing for the launch of the Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST), rigorously testing early data-processing pipelines using DP0.2 galaxy catalogs. By tailoring the CMNN PhotoZ algorithm for LSST data products, I produced accurate photometric redshift estimates without spectroscopic inputs—a capability that will be essential once LSST begins generating its unprecedented volume of observations.

In addition to redshift estimation, I developed methods to associate simulated Type Ia supernovae with their likely host galaxies, supporting the broader goal of enabling reliable transient–host matching for early LSST science. Through this work, I gained experience with large-scale astronomical datasets, machine-learning methodologies, and pipeline development for next-generation survey science. Results from this project were presented at both AAS and APS meetings.

FOAMS: Chemical Evolution Modeling

As part of Dr. Brian Morsony’s Fitting Observed Abundances to Metals from Supernovae (FOAMS) project, I investigated the relative contributions of Type Ia and core-collapse supernovae (CCSNe) to the chemical enrichment of the Milky Way. The FOAMS framework compares observed stellar abundances with theoretical nucleosynthesis yields, enabling constraints on the underlying supernova progenitor populations that shape Galactic chemical evolution.

My role centered on assembling and processing large stellar abundance datasets from the APOGEE survey. I curated elemental abundance measurements across the Milky Way and fit them to nucleosynthesis models using the FOAMS pipeline to infer the ratios of Type Ia to CCSNe progenitors. This effort culminated in the construction of a catalog that maps these supernova population ratios throughout the Galaxy. With this foundational dataset prepared, the next phase will focus on identifying which chemical elements serve as the most reliable tracers of the underlying supernova mix.

Through this project, I strengthened my skills in statistical analysis, computational modeling, and the interpretation of complex astrophysical datasets. The experience deepened my understanding of Galactic chemical evolution and reinforced my commitment to a research career in astrophysics.
