About Me

I am a Ph.D. student at Michigan State University studying compact objects, relativistic phenomena, and the explosive deaths of massive stars. My research bridges high-energy astrophysics, numerical simulations, and observable cosmic signatures.

Previously, at CUNY for my master’s degree, I studied core-collapse supernovae collapsing into black holes using 3D GRMHD simulations. My work showed that even non-spinning black holes can produce subrelativistic outflows via accretion-disk dynamics, unbinding the stellar envelope and connecting spherical core-collapse to relativistic gamma-ray bursts. This formed my master’s thesis and first first-author publication.

I’m also passionate about mentorship and outreach, working with programs like AstroCom NYC and MSU Stellar Mentorship, and organizing public events like CSUS Astronomy Night to make astrophysics accessible and inspire future scientists.
