About Me

Background, interests, and current work

I am a PhD student in Astrophysics and Computational Mathematics, Science and Engineering at Michigan State University, where I work with Dr. Sean Couch on compact objects, stellar collapse, and black hole formation.

Biography

I mostly think about how massive stars collapse, how black holes form, and how accretion flows and outflows develop in extreme environments. My work is mainly theoretical and computational, and I like projects where the physics is complicated enough that you really need simulations to see what is going on.

Before MSU, I completed my master's degree at CUNY, where I worked with Dr. Ore Gottlieb at the Center for Computational Astrophysics at the Flatiron Institute. There I analyzed 3D GRMHD simulations of collapsars, focusing on how accretion-disk dynamics can generate outflows even in the absence of black hole spin.

I have also worked on Rubin survey science with Dr. Brian Morsony, which was a useful project and gave me some experience with large survey-style data analysis, but my main direction is still computational astrophysics. In general, I enjoy the mix of simulation work, physical interpretation, and figuring out how to communicate complicated results clearly.

Current Position

PhD student in Astrophysics and CMSE at Michigan State University, currently working with Dr. Sean Couch.

Research Interests

  • Core-collapse supernovae and compact object formation
  • GRMHD and computational astrophysics
  • Accretion disks, jets, and outflows
  • Black hole formation in 1D and 3D simulations
  • Theoretical high-energy astrophysics

Methods and Tools

  • High-performance scientific computing
  • GRMHD simulations with HAMR
  • 1D stellar collapse studies with GR1D
  • Data analysis and scientific visualization in Python

Mentorship and Community

I care a lot about mentorship, accessibility, and representation in STEM. Through programs such as AstroCom NYC, MSU Stellar Mentorship, and astronomy outreach efforts, I try to contribute to a more welcoming and supportive astrophysics community.

Long term, I hope to build a career in academia that includes research, teaching, and real community-building.
