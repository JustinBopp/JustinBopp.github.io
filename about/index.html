About Me

Background, interests, and current work

I am a PhD student in Astrophysics and Computational Mathematics, Science and Engineering at Michigan State University, where I study compact objects, stellar explosions, black hole formation, and the numerical physics of extreme environments.

Biography

My work is primarily in theoretical and computational astrophysics, with a focus on compact object formation, core-collapse supernovae, accretion flows, and disk-driven outflows. I am especially interested in how numerical simulations can connect the physics of stellar collapse to the engines that power energetic transients.

Before MSU, I completed my master's degree at CUNY, where I worked with Dr. Ore Gottlieb at the Center for Computational Astrophysics at the Flatiron Institute. There I analyzed 3D GRMHD simulations of collapsars, focusing on how accretion-disk dynamics can generate outflows even in the absence of black hole spin.

More broadly, I am interested in combining large-scale simulations, careful physical interpretation, and clear scientific communication. One of my past projects involved Rubin survey science, but my main research direction remains theoretical and computational astrophysics.

Current Position

Mentorship and Community

Alongside research, I care deeply about mentorship, accessibility, and representation in STEM. Through programs such as AstroCom NYC, MSU Stellar Mentorship, and astronomy outreach efforts, I try to help build a more inclusive astrophysics community.

My long-term goal is a career in academia that combines research, teaching, and community building.
