About Me

Background, interests, and current work

I am a PhD student in Astrophysics and Computational Mathematics, Science and Engineering at Michigan State University, where I study compact objects, stellar explosions, and the extreme physics of energetic transients.

Short Bio

My research sits at the intersection of high-energy astrophysics, numerical simulations, and observational signatures of transient phenomena. Before MSU, I completed my master's degree at CUNY, where I worked with Dr. Ore Gottlieb at the Center for Computational Astrophysics at the Flatiron Institute.

There I analyzed 3D GRMHD simulations of collapsars, focusing on how accretion-disk dynamics can generate outflows even in the absence of black hole spin. That work shaped my continuing interest in compact object formation and the connection between engines, outflows, and observable transients.

Research Interests

  • Core-collapse supernovae and compact object formation
  • GRMHD and computational astrophysics
  • Accretion disks, jets, and outflows
  • Observational signatures of energetic transients

Mentorship and Community

Alongside research, I care deeply about mentorship, accessibility, and representation in STEM. Through programs such as AstroCom NYC, MSU Stellar Mentorship, and astronomy outreach efforts, I try to help build a more inclusive astrophysics community.

My long-term goal is a career in academia that combines research, teaching, and community building.
