About Me

Background, interests, and current work

I am a PhD student in Astrophysics and Computational Mathematics, Science and Engineering at Michigan State University, where I work with Dr. Sean Couch on compact objects, stellar collapse, and black hole formation.

Biography

I am originally from Modesto, California, and I first got into astrophysics while studying physics at California State University, Stanislaus. That was where I started thinking more seriously about astronomy and high-energy astrophysics, and it set me on the path toward graduate work in computational astrophysics.

I completed my master's degree at the City University of New York, Graduate Center in New York City through the MS Astrophysics Bridge Program, where I worked with Dr. Ore Gottlieb at the Center for Computational Astrophysics at the Flatiron Institute. My master's work focused on 3D GRMHD simulations of collapsars and disk outflows, which gave me a strong foundation in relativistic simulations and large-scale computational work.

At Michigan State University, my work is centered on core-collapse supernovae and the full sequence from stellar collapse to black hole formation. I work on problems that require realistic physics and large-scale simulation, including neutrino transport, compact-object formation, and 3D GRMHD modeling in extreme environments. A major focus of my current research is understanding how massive stars collapse, how black holes and relativistic outflows emerge, and how these systems connect stellar explosions with the production of heavy elements.

Outside of research, I still carry a lot from growing up in 4-H and FFA, and I value mentoring, teaching, and making science feel more accessible. I live with my partner Andrea and our dog Goliath.

Research Interests

  • Core-collapse supernovae and compact object formation
  • GRMHD and computational astrophysics
  • Accretion disks, jets, and outflows
  • Black hole formation in 1D and 3D simulations
  • Theoretical high-energy astrophysics

Methods and Tools

  • High-performance scientific computing
  • GRMHD simulations with HAMR
  • 1D stellar collapse studies with GR1D
  • Data analysis and scientific visualization in Python
